Summary

We present a modified version of VampNet that converts human hummings into musical audio. The key modifications made to VampNet lie in the masking and sampling processes. This method exhibits strong performance in short audio inputs and has natural variation in instruments, genre, and pace of the output whilst consistently matching the input melodies.

Motivation

Our project was exploring Style Transfer using vampnet. Mostly, we just wanted to play with vampnet. But also, we were motivated by thinking of music generation as serving two big groups: musicians and consumers. For musicians, we want generative tools to offer them new ways to play with their own style. We want to enable them to express themselves in modalities that are different or more direct than what they had before. The tool shouldn't be a barrier or constraint. We also think an ideal tool is accessible enough to empower users who want to enhance their consumption or move into something more creative entirely.Turntables, mixtapes, and Garageband come to mind. Tools like Shazam in combination with social media changed how we consumed and discussed music.

The mode of expression we felt was most essential and therefore the most natural interface for a generative tool like this was pure melody, in the form of humming or whistling. While the precise sonic qualities that differentiate pieces of music from each other are often hard to pin down, melody is probably what we consider most definitive. If you have to describe a song to someone, simply humming it is easier than trying to come up with verbal explanations or clapping the rhythm of different instruments in the song. From a musician and consumer perspective, this is a really natural way to express the core of a musical idea. We felt we could leverage vampnet's generative capabilities to expand on these ideas in a way that might be interesting to the users and beyond anything they could achieve with a voice transformer over some simple humming.

How it works

Masking

In order to mask the input audio so as to conserve the orignal audio's melody whilst giving vampnet creative freedom, we tried different masking techniques. The best results were derived when the masking model worked on a linear schedule. This masked 90% of the lowest layer of the coarse tokens randomly to 10% of the topmost layer of the fine tokens.
